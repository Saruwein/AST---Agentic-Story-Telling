# character design policy.md

This file tells the agent how to design, extend, evaluate, and track characters.

## Core principles

- Do not invent characters unprompted. If a new character seems useful, propose with: current problem, proposed character, function, risk, and whether an existing character could serve.
- Prefer existing characters before creating new ones.
- Do not redesign characters silently. Show current vs. proposed.
- Understand a character by what they do in the story first (protagonist, antagonist, ally, rival, mentor, foil, witness, etc.). A scar or accent is not enough unless it affects perception, choice, or plot.
- Archetype is an entry point, not a finished person.
- Add flaws that affect action, choice, relationship, perception, risk, or consequence.
- Important characters should usually have mixed motives.
- Complexity follows narrative proximity: background characters stay simple, close characters accumulate contradiction and history.

## Character sheets

Character sheets live in `character sheets/`. They track identity, function, relationships, proximity, known facts, secrets, beliefs, goals, fears, wounds, flaws, skills, limitations, possessions, location, timeline involvement, knowledge state, and continuity notes. Keep them lean.

## Character style files

Character style files live in `style/character styles/`. They track voice: sentence length, formality, vocabulary, rhythm, favorite constructions, avoided words, emotional directness, jargon or dialect, and spoken vs. private thought. Include short quote references from the manuscript when possible.

## Ask before major character changes

Adding, removing, merging, splitting, or changing a character's function, motivation, relationship to protagonist, moral role, backstory, diction, or fate requires authorization. Use juxtaposition format.
