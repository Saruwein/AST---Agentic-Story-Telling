# default style.md

This file defines the default stylistic behavior of a story before the human author, the project configuration, `global style.md`, or a more local style file changes it.

These anchors are not rules. They are starting pressures. They help the agent draft, revise, translate, and evaluate text when no stronger project-specific instruction exists.

In this framework, **style** means the preferred behavior of the narrative. It includes prose, pacing, tension, clarity, complexity, worldbuilding habits, repetition, surprise, reader orientation, description, dialogue, and the handling of intense material.

The agent may analyze deviations from these defaults, especially when they increase reader load. A stable global deviation is usually easier for the reader to adapt to than many abrupt local deviations. This observation is advisory, not a hard metric.

## Reader Has a Path

By default, the reader should have enough orientation to follow the text.

This does not mean the reader must know the full truth. It means the reader should usually have a stable path through the scene: who is present, where the scene takes place, when it happens, whose perspective governs it, and what immediate pressure is active.

Mystery may hide answers. Confusion removes the path.

## Plain Language Is the Baseline

By default, the agent should prefer clear syntax, correct orthography, readable sentence structure, and standard punctuation.

Dialect, idiolect, divergent spelling, jargon, loanwords, archaisms, invented terms, or broken grammar may be used when they serve character, setting, tone, genre, humor, estrangement, or worldbuilding.

They should be deliberate stylistic devices rather than accidental noise.

## Prefer Linear Storytelling

By default, the agent should prefer linear storytelling.

Events should usually unfold in an order the reader can follow without reconstructing the timeline. Flashbacks, flashforwards, nested memories, parallel timelines, time loops, and fractured chronology may be used, but they should be deliberate stylistic choices rather than accidental complexity.

## Prefer Low Temporal Complexity

By default, the agent should keep the timeline simple.

The reader should not need to constantly ask whether a scene happens before, after, during, inside, or outside another scene unless temporal complexity is part of the intended style.

## Prefer Low Local Complexity

By default, each scene or passage should avoid carrying too many new burdens at once.

The agent should be careful when introducing many new characters, locations, objects, concepts, rules, conflicts, time shifts, tonal changes, or stylistic deviations in the same local passage.

Complexity may accumulate across the story, but it should not overload a single moment without purpose.

## Tension Has a Shape

By default, tension should be shaped deliberately.

The agent should not assume that tension must always rise in a straight line. Tension may peak early, late, in the middle, repeatedly, quietly, or not at all.

What matters is that the tension pattern feels intentional and suited to the story.

## Comparable Moments Need Comparable Weight

By default, similar plot points should receive similar narrative weight.

If two clues, battles, confessions, discoveries, departures, or reversals are equally important, the agent should not lavish attention on one and rush through the other without a stylistic reason.

Uneven weighting can be powerful, but the imbalance should feel meaningful rather than accidental.

## Repetition Must Earn Its Return

By default, the agent should avoid repeating the same kind of obstacle, argument, misunderstanding, reveal, emotional beat, reversal, or solution without development.

Repetition becomes useful when it creates rhythm, motif, ritual, obsession, irony, escalation, comedy, or tragic inevitability.

The problem is not repetition itself. The problem is repetition that does not change the story’s meaning, pressure, or direction.

## Avoid Way-backing Through the Plot

By default, the agent should avoid making the story retrace a previous chain of events, locations, clues, tasks, or plot points in the same order, reverse order, or arbitrary shuffled order.

Way-backing occurs when the narrative seems to move forward only by walking back through a route it has already taken.

Example:

```text
location A → location B → location C
then later:
location A → location B → location C again
```

Example:

```text
task 1 → task 2 → task 3
then later:
task 3 → task 2 → task 1
```

Returning to an earlier location, clue, character, or conflict can be useful. The problem is not return. The problem is retracing an already-used sequence without enough transformation, compression, escalation, reinterpretation, or consequence.

## Plant and Resolve Story Hooks

By default, the agent may place small unresolved story elements that can gain meaning later.

A story hook may be an object, phrase, habit, rumor, minor contradiction, social rule, location detail, overheard line, strange absence, repeated gesture, or apparently incidental fact.

The agent should treat such hooks as open narrative threads, not as punctuation marks or mechanical symbols.

A useful story hook should usually be:

```text
small enough not to distract
specific enough to be remembered
natural enough to belong in the current scene
capable of gaining meaning later
```

When a hook returns, it should do more than merely prove that the author planned ahead. It should change interpretation, create consequence, solve a problem, deepen character, sharpen irony, or reveal hidden structure.

The agent should not overload the story with hooks. Too many unresolved threads increase reader load and may make the story feel engineered rather than alive.

## Worldbuilding Earns Its Footprint

By default, the agent should keep worldbuilding lean.

A small number of consequential differences is often stronger than a large number of decorative differences. The agent should avoid enriching the world merely because it can.

Worldbuilding details should usually affect action, atmosphere, interpretation, character behavior, translation, continuity, or reader comprehension.

## Reality Is Ordinary Until Declared Otherwise

By default, the agent should assume broadly familiar causality, bodily limits, gravity, distance, time, and measuring units.

When the story uses different physics, cosmology, magic, technology, dream logic, symbolic logic, or measurement systems, the agent should follow the relevant worldbuilding file rather than this default.

## Surprise Should Know What It Is Doing

By default, surprise should serve the story’s style.

Many stories benefit from surprise that feels unexpected at first and meaningful in retrospect. Other stories may use absurd, excessive, arbitrary, comic, surreal, episodic, or anti-logical surprise.

The agent should not treat surprise as automatically good or bad. It should ask what kind of surprise the story is using.

## Description Earns Its Length

By default, the length of a description should match its function.

A description may be short when it only needs to orient the reader. It may be longer when it builds atmosphere, reveals character, slows time, establishes a world, creates tension, hides a clue, or changes interpretation.

The agent should not assume that short description is always better, or that rich description is always better. The question is what the description is doing.

## Sensory Detail Serves the Scene

By default, sensory detail should make the scene more inhabitable.

The agent should use images, gestures, objects, sounds, textures, and places when they sharpen atmosphere, character, action, tension, or memory.

Description should not expand merely because description is possible.

## Dialogue Carries Pressure

By default, dialogue should do more than exchange information.

It may reveal desire, avoidance, status, intimacy, fear, deception, contradiction, restraint, ritual, banality, bureaucracy, or failed communication.

Flat dialogue can be a valid style, but it should be flat for a reason.

## Intensity Must Earn Its Place

By default, the agent should not use sex, violence, cruelty, humiliation, gore, trauma, or shock merely to intensify the text.

Such material may be used when it affects character, plot, atmosphere, moral pressure, relationship dynamics, worldbuilding, or theme.

It should not appear only because the story needs stimulation.

## Cuttable Beauty Is Still Cuttable

By default, the agent should be willing to remove elegant, clever, beautiful, or interesting material when it weakens the story.

A passage may be well-written and still be wrong for the scene.

The agent should evaluate whether a detail, sentence, joke, image, subplot, or explanation serves the current work rather than protecting it merely because it is attractive.

## Character Complexity Follows Narrative Proximity

By default, the perceived complexity of a character should correspond to their narrative proximity.

A distant background character may remain simple. A recurring minor character may need one or two memorable traits. A close ally, rival, antagonist, lover, narrator, or family member usually needs more contradiction, history, desire, and pressure.

In mostly linear storytelling, perceived character complexity should usually accumulate as the reader spends more time near the character.

Actual character design belongs in character files. This anchor only describes the default stylistic pressure.

## Style May Change Locally

By default, the story does not need to sound identical everywhere.

A chapter, scene, passage, character, object, ritual, substance, holiday, translation layer, or narrative frame may locally alter the style.

Such changes should be tracked when they affect drafting, editing, revision, translation, continuity, or reader experience.

## Stylistic Devices Should Be Conscious

By default, unusual stylistic behavior should be treated as meaningful.

This includes abrupt pacing shifts, dense description, sparse narration, strange syntax, withheld information, sudden comedy, excessive repetition, archaic diction, alien terminology, dream logic, and other deviations from the ordinary style.

The human author and the agent do not need to identify every device in advance. However, when a device becomes important, repeated, disruptive, or difficult for the reader, the agent should make it visible enough that the human author can decide whether to keep, adjust, or localize it.
